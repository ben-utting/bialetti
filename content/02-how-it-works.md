# How the Moka Pot Works

## The Three Chambers

Disassemble any Moka pot and you have three distinct sections:

| Chamber | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Bottom (boiler)** | Holds water. Sealed, it becomes a pressure vessel. |
| **Middle (basket + funnel)** | Holds the ground coffee. The funnel channels water upward from below. |
| **Top (collector)** | Receives the brewed coffee. This is what you pour from. |

The brilliance of the design is that the brew moves *upward* — the opposite direction to a V60 or pour-over. Water is pushed through the coffee from below, not gravity-fed from above.

---

## The Physics: What Actually Happens

When you seal the pot and apply heat:

1. **The water heats up.** Even below boiling point, water evaporates slightly. This evaporation creates vapour pressure inside the sealed boiler.

2. **Pressure builds.** As temperature rises, evaporation increases, pressure increases. You don't need to reach 100°C to start building pressure — it happens progressively.

3. **Water is pushed through the funnel.** Once there's enough pressure, the water is forced up through the funnel tube, into the coffee basket, and through the grounds.

4. **Coffee brews.** Hot, pressurised water extracts compounds from the coffee as it passes through.

5. **Brewed coffee rises into the top chamber.** It travels up the central spout and fills the collector from the bottom.

At around **0.3 bar** of pressure, liquid typically begins to flow through the coffee — this is the critical moment for controlling the brew.

---

## The Safety Valve

The safety valve on the lower chamber is not decorative. If the filter basket becomes blocked and water cannot pass through, pressure would continue to build dangerously. The valve opens at approximately **3 bar** to release pressure safely. Never cover or obstruct it.

---

## Brew Temperature: The Core Challenge

This is where it gets interesting. Because the water is in a sealed, pressurised vessel, it can exceed 100°C — water only boils at exactly 100°C at atmospheric pressure. Under pressure, the boiling point rises.

In testing using instrumented pots with temperature probes and pressure sensors, brew temperatures were recorded ranging from:

- **~88–92°C** with properly controlled technique using freshly boiled water
- Up to **120–130°C** in uncontrolled, high-heat situations

The higher end of that range produces harsh, bitter coffee with a characteristic burnt quality. The goal of good technique is to keep brew temperatures in the lower range.

**Starting water temperature matters significantly:**
- Starting with cold water (20°C): first water through coffee ≈ 60°C (rises to ~120°C by end)
- Starting with freshly boiled water: first water through coffee ≈ 88–92°C (much more controlled)

Starting hot means a more consistent, controlled brew from first drop to last.

---

## The Sputtering Phase

Near the end of a brew, you may hear an angry gurgling or see coffee spurting. This is **uncondensed steam passing directly through the coffee puck** into the top chamber. 

This phase correlates strongly with:
- Very high brew temperatures
- Bitter, harsh flavour in the cup
- The boiler running low on liquid water

The goal is to stop the brew before this phase begins. Once it starts, the coffee already in the top chamber is largely fine — but any further extraction happening at this point will add bitterness.

---

## Why It Isn't Espresso

People often call Moka pot coffee "stovetop espresso." It isn't — though the confusion is understandable.

| | Moka Pot | Espresso |
|---|---|---|
| Pressure | ~1–2 bar | 9 bar |
| Grind | Medium-fine | Very fine |
| Water ratio | Higher (≈10:1) | Lower (≈2:1) |
| Crema | None (some foam) | Yes |
| Temperature | 88–120°C (variable) | ~93°C (controlled) |

The Moka produces a strong, concentrated, metal-filtered coffee with a thick texture and rich flavour. It is its own thing — not a poor imitation of espresso.

## Aluminium vs Stainless Steel

Bialetti's classic pots are aluminium. There is **no credible scientific evidence** linking aluminium cookware to Alzheimer's disease — this has been investigated and dismissed by Alzheimer's research organisations.

From a purely thermal performance perspective, stainless steel appears to perform slightly better in controlled testing:
- Aluminium conducts heat more evenly, but this can mean the lower chamber gets *very* hot
- Stainless seems to insulate the water slightly from aggressive heat, resulting in more moderate brew temperatures
- The difference is real but not dramatic

The classic aluminium pot works perfectly well with good technique. A stainless steel pot offers a slightly more forgiving heat profile.

## The Myth of the Patina

A common piece of received wisdom says you should never fully clean a Moka pot — that the build-up of old coffee residue ("seasoning" or "patina") improves the flavour.

It does not. Old coffee residue is rancid fat and oxidised compounds. It adds staleness and bitterness, not character. Clean your pot thoroughly after every use with warm water (no soap — soap can leave residue in the gasket and affect taste over time), and dry it completely before storing.
