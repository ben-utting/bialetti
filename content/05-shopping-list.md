# Shopping List & Upgrades

> Items that will genuinely improve your brews — ranked by impact. None of these are essential, but each one earns its place.

---

## Tier 1: High Impact, Low Cost

### Kitchen Scales (Digital, 0.1g precision)
**Why:** Measuring your coffee dose and your yield (how much brewed coffee you're getting out) is the most useful diagnostic tool you have. Once you know what "full basket" weighs for your grinder, and you can see whether you're hitting your target yield of 130–150ml, you'll be able to diagnose and fix problems much faster.

**What to look for:** 0.1g precision, at least 500g capacity, simple tare function. Nothing fancy needed.

**Approximate cost:** £10–20

---

### Cast Iron or Stainless Diffusion Plate (Flame Spreader)
**Why:** This is the single most impactful accessory for a gas hob. It sits between the flame and your Moka pot and acts as a thermal buffer. When you cut the gas, the plate keeps gently releasing heat into the pot — enough to sustain the brew without spiking temperature. It turns a finicky process into a more forgiving one.

Also useful for safety: it gives the pot a stable, flat surface to sit on over a gas burner.

**What to look for:** Cast iron preferred (holds heat well). Should be small enough to focus heat under the Moka pot base.

**Approximate cost:** £10–25 (or check existing kitchen equipment — some griddle pans work well)

---

### Aeropress Paper Filters (Standard Round)
**Why:** One filter placed inside the top section of the Moka pot (sitting against the metal filter plate) noticeably improves clarity and sweetness in the cup. The paper catches fine particles and oils that the metal filter lets through, producing a cleaner extraction.

**What to look for:** Standard Aeropress filters (the round ones). One box of 350 will last you years at the cost of a few pounds.

**Approximate cost:** £5–8 for 350 filters

---

## Tier 2: Meaningful Upgrade, Moderate Cost

### Burr Grinder (Manual or Electric)
**Why:** If you're currently using a blade grinder (the spinning blade type), upgrading to a burr grinder is the single biggest quality improvement you can make — bigger than any technique change. Blade grinders produce wildly inconsistent particle sizes: some too coarse, some powdery fine. The fine particles over-extract and add bitterness; the coarse particles under-extract and add sourness. Both are in your cup simultaneously.

A burr grinder produces a consistent particle size, which means consistent extraction.

**Manual options (lower cost, good quality):**
- Timemore C2 / C3 (~£45–65) — excellent value
- 1Zpresso Q2 / JX (~£60–90) — superb grind quality for the price

**Electric options:**
- Baratza Encore (~£130–160) — the classic starter electric burr grinder
- Wilfa Svart / Uniform (~£100–130) — excellent Scandinavian design

**Grind setting for Moka:** Medium-fine. Finer than filter coffee, coarser than espresso.

---

### Replacement Gasket + Spare Filters
**Why:** The rubber gasket (the seal between the top and bottom chambers) degrades over time. A cracked or hardened gasket causes leaks around the join and can affect the seal's ability to hold pressure. Having a spare means you're never caught out.

Bialetti sells official replacement gasket + filter sets. They're cheap and it's worth having one in reserve.

**What to look for:** "Bialetti 4-cup replacement gasket" — make sure you match the cup size.

**Approximate cost:** £5–10

---

## Tier 3: Nice to Have

### WDT Needle Tool (Distribution Tool)
**Why:** A WDT (Weiss Distribution Technique) tool is a handle with thin needles attached. You use it to stir the ground coffee in the basket, breaking up clumps and improving even distribution. Clumps can cause channelling — water finding a path of least resistance through a dense clump rather than extracting evenly.

For a Moka pot, the impact is real but modest compared to an espresso machine. A quick stir and tap to settle is often sufficient without one.

**What to look for:** Any barista WDT tool with fine needles, or DIY versions using toothpick handles with acupuncture needles.

**Approximate cost:** £8–20

---

### Measuring Jug or Scale-Under-Pot Approach
**Why:** Checking your yield after each brew tells you exactly how your technique is performing. A small measuring jug (with ml markings) means you can pour and check without needing a scale.

Target: 130–150ml from a 4-cup pot.

**Approximate cost:** £3–8

## What You Don't Need

- A tamper — don't tamp a Moka pot basket
- Pressurised coffee capsules or pods designed for Moka pots — these compromise flavour
- Pre-ground supermarket coffee described as "Moka grind" — fresh-ground is significantly better
- An expensive pressure gauge — useful for deep experimentation, not for daily brewing
