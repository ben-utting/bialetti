# The Story of the Bialetti Moka Express

## Origins: 1919–1933

Alfonso Bialetti returned from a metalworking apprenticeship in France and founded his aluminium company in 1919 in Crusinallo, a small town in northern Italy near the alpine lake district. Fourteen years later, in 1933, came the invention that would make the Bialetti name known in virtually every corner of the world: the Moka Express.

The exact story of its invention is a little murky. Alfonso's name is on the patent, but a figure named **Luigi De Ponti** — an engineer or inventor working for Bialetti — is also closely associated with the design. The two are likely co-inventors, though it's difficult to verify without access to the original 1933 patent filing, which is surprisingly hard to locate.

What is clear is the inspiration behind it: earlier steam-powered coffee devices (some dating back to 1878) used steam to *move* water above coffee, producing something closer to a poured brew. The real innovation of the Moka Express was using steam pressure to *drive* water **through** the coffee — a simpler, faster, more intense extraction. The original prototype closely resembled the design we know today, though the base was narrower.

---

## The Renato Era: 1945 Onwards

The transformation of Bialetti from a small Italian manufacturer into a global icon owes much to Alfonso's son, **Renato Bialetti**. He returned from German prisoner-of-war camps in 1945 and took over the family business, refocusing it entirely around the Moka Express.

Under Renato's direction, the design was refined:

- The **wider, octagonal base** was introduced in the 1950s to improve heat capture from the stove beneath.
- The **Bakelite handle** was formalised — heat-resistant, practical, and distinctively shaped.
- The **domed lid** with its small knob became the silhouette the world now recognises.

These refinements were patented in the 1950s, giving the pot the exact shape it still holds today.

---

## L'Omino con i Baffi

In the 1950s, Bialetti hired a cartoonist to create what became one of Italy's most recognisable brand icons: **l'omino con i baffi** — the little man with the moustache. Supposedly modelled on Renato himself (the resemblance is believable), the character became synonymous with the brand and remains on every pot sold today.

Bialetti's television advertising ran in a programme called *Carosello* — a ten-minute variety show broadcast after the evening news in Italy, with audiences of up to ten million. The messaging was consistent and reassuring: *coffee, water, fire — and a Moka Express.* It was enormously effective.

---

## Cultural Penetration

One study found that **nine out of every ten Italian households** owns a Moka Express. By various estimates, Bialetti has sold somewhere between **200 and 300 million** pots over the decades. Add in pots made by other manufacturers — including Alessi, also founded in the same town around the same time — and the number of Moka-style pots in existence is staggering.

---

## Difficult Decades

The company changed hands several times. In 1986 it was sold to the FAMA group (espresso machine manufacturers). In 1993 it moved to Rondò Italia (cookware). The two eventually merged into **Bialetti Industrie**, which was listed on the Italian stock market.

Renato Bialetti passed away in 2016 at the age of 93. In accordance with his wishes, his ashes were interred not in an urn, but in a large Moka pot.

By 2018, the company had accumulated over €70 million in debt and declared bankruptcy. It secured a near-€40 million loan from the American hedge fund Sculptor to continue trading. Competition from pod machines — particularly within Italy — had taken a significant toll.

---

## The Enduring Object

Whatever happens to Bialetti as a company, the object itself seems destined to outlast any corporate chapter. The Moka Express is arguably the most recognisable coffee brewer ever made — identifiable in silhouette from any angle. It is shorthand for coffee itself in a way that no other brewer has achieved.

It is woven into family life, into memory, into morning routines across the world. That doesn't disappear with a balance sheet.
