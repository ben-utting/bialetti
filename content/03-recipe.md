# Your Recipe: 4-Cup Bialetti on Gas

> **Your setup:** 4-cup aluminium Bialetti · Gas hob · No tamper · No WDT needle tool
> 
> This recipe is calibrated to your exact equipment. Follow the constants every time; adjust the variables as you dial in.

---

## The Goal

Get at least **60% of your boiler water through the coffee** before the pot starts sputtering. For your 4-cup pot, that means roughly 130–150ml of brewed coffee in the top chamber. This gives you full extraction control and avoids the harsh, bitter flavours that come from an overheated finish.

---

## What You'll Need

- 4-cup Bialetti Moka pot
- Kettle (boiled)
- Kitchen scales (ideally, but not essential)
- Medium-fine ground coffee (freshly ground if possible)
- Small towel or cloth (to handle the hot base)
- Your gas hob on its **lowest possible flame**

---

## Constants — Do These Every Single Brew

These apply regardless of which coffee you're using.

### 1. Start with freshly boiled water in the base

Never put cold water in the boiler. Fill to **just below the safety valve** with water straight from the kettle. This gives you a controlled starting temperature (~85–90°C in the pot after the transfer) and a faster, more consistent brew.

**Why it matters:** Cold water in the base means the first water to hit the coffee is only around 60°C — massively under-extracting the early brew — while the last water runs dangerously hot.

### 2. Fill the basket fully (but don't tamp)

Fill the coffee basket to the top, level it off gently, and **do not press it down**. You have no tamper and don't need one — the Moka pot doesn't require a compressed puck the way espresso does. Compressing the coffee too tightly increases resistance, raises brew temperature, and risks uneven extraction.

Give the basket a gentle horizontal tap on the counter to settle the grounds naturally. That's enough.

**Target dose:** Approximately 20–22g of coffee for the 4-cup (weigh once to understand what "full basket" looks like for your grinder, then use that as your reference).

### 3. Keep the lid open while brewing

Leave the lid up so you can watch and listen. You need to see the moment liquid begins to appear in the top chamber, and hear the moment sputtering starts. You can't do either with the lid down.

### 4. Stop before the sputter

The moment you hear that angry gurgling, remove the pot from the heat immediately and run the base under the cold tap. This stops the brew instantly by cooling the boiler and collapsing the pressure.

Ideally you stop just *before* this point — but the cold tap is your emergency brake.

## Your Technique: Gas Hob Method

### Step 1 — Boil your kettle

While it's boiling, disassemble your pot and set it out.

### Step 2 — Fill the basket with coffee

Fill to the top, tap once to settle. No tamping.

### Step 3 — Fill the base with boiled water

Fill to just below the safety valve. Use a cloth to hold the base — it'll be hot.

### Step 4 — Assemble the pot

Screw the top and bottom together firmly. The cloth helps here too.

### Step 5 — Put it on the lowest flame

Place it on your smallest burner at its lowest setting. Centre the pot over the flame. The lower the heat, the better the temperature control.

### Step 6 — Watch and wait

Keep an eye on the top chamber. After a few minutes (typically 3–5 on low gas), liquid will start to appear — first slowly, then building to a steady flow.

**As soon as liquid starts flowing:** Turn the gas off completely.

The residual heat in the pot and the burner grate will continue to supply enough energy to finish the brew. On a 4-cup pot this usually works well — there's less thermal mass than a larger pot, so the existing heat is sufficient.

### Step 7 — Monitor the flow

Watch the flow continue. It should be slow and steady — not gushing, not trickling to a stop. 

- If it slows too much and seems to stall: briefly turn the gas back on very low for 10–15 seconds, then off again.
- If it suddenly speeds up: lift the pot off the grate entirely for a few seconds.

### Step 8 — Stop at the right moment

When you have a good amount of coffee in the top chamber and *before* you hear the first sputter, remove the pot from the heat. If you hear sputtering: cold tap immediately.

Check your yield: you want approximately **130–150ml** out of a 4-cup pot (from roughly 200–230ml of water in the base). If you're consistently getting much less before sputtering starts, see the troubleshooting section.

### Step 9 — Pour immediately

Don't leave the coffee sitting in the hot aluminium top chamber. Pour it straight away. The pot retains a lot of heat and the coffee will continue to "cook" if left in it.

## Variables to Adjust

### Grind size

This is your primary dial.

| Coffee roast | Grind target |
|---|---|
| Light roast | Fine — finer than filter, approaching (but not at) espresso |
| Medium roast | Medium-fine — similar to fine filter |
| Dark roast | Medium — closer to a coarse filter grind |

Lighter roasts need finer grinding to extract well. Darker roasts are more soluble and extract easily — a coarser grind keeps the brew cooler and prevents over-extraction.

If your brew is:
- **Too sour / thin** → grind finer (or fill the boiler more)
- **Too bitter / harsh** → grind coarser (or reduce heat, or fill boiler less)

### How full to fill the boiler

- **Light roast:** Fill right up to the base of the safety valve — you want maximum water to extract those harder-to-reach flavours.
- **Dark roast:** Fill to about 2/3–3/4 full. Less water = slightly lower temperature, and you don't need as much liquid to fully extract a dark roast.

### Diluting the final cup

Moka pot coffee is strong — typically 3–4% dissolved solids, far stronger than filter coffee. If your brew tastes well-extracted but just overwhelming in intensity or acidity, try adding **80–100ml of freshly boiled water** to your cup. This makes a "Moka Americano" and often reveals remarkable sweetness and balance that was hidden under the intensity.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Coffee spurts out early (low yield) | Heat too high, or grind too fine | Use lowest flame; grind slightly coarser |
| Coffee tastes sour/flat | Under-extracted (too coarse, or low yield) | Grind finer; fill boiler more; ensure full basket |
| Coffee tastes bitter/burnt | Over-heated | Lower the flame; stop earlier; cold tap sooner |
| Flow stalls and stops | Heat too low, or grind too coarse | Briefly re-apply low heat |
| Very inconsistent results | Grind inconsistency | Invest in a burr grinder if using blade grinder |

## A Note on Cleaning

After each brew, once the pot is cool:
- Disassemble fully
- Rinse all parts under warm water — no dish soap needed (a small amount is fine occasionally, just rinse well)
- Check the gasket (rubber seal) periodically — it should be soft and flexible, not cracked or hard
- Dry all parts before reassembling or storing

Replace the gasket when it shows signs of cracking or when you notice the pot leaking around the join.
