# Brew Log & Troubleshooting

## How to Use the Brew Log

Logging a few key variables after each brew is the fastest way to improve. You don't need to log everything — pick the variables you're currently trying to dial in. A few weeks of notes will reveal patterns that tasting alone won't.

## Tasting Framework

After each brew, run through these four questions:

### 1. Bitterness
*Scale: None / Mild / Moderate / Harsh*

Bitterness that lingers or burns at the back of the throat is a sign of over-extraction or excessive heat. A mild, pleasant bitterness is fine and expected.

### 2. Sourness / Acidity
*Scale: None / Pleasant / Sharp / Sour*

Some acidity is desirable — it provides brightness. Sour, sharp, or unpleasant acidity usually indicates under-extraction. Try diluting with a little hot water before concluding it's unsalvageable — sometimes the acidity is extracted but just overwhelmingly concentrated.

### 3. Sweetness
*Scale: None / Faint / Present / Prominent*

A well-extracted Moka brew should have clear sweetness. Lack of sweetness can indicate either under-extraction (not enough) or over-extraction (sweetness masked by bitterness).

### 4. Body / Texture
*Scale: Thin / Medium / Full / Heavy*

Moka coffee has more body than filter coffee due to the metal filtration and concentration. Very thin body often suggests low yield or coarse grinding.

## Troubleshooting Quick Reference

### "Coffee tastes bitter and harsh"

Most likely causes (in order of likelihood):
1. **Heat too high** — brew temperature got too hot, especially towards the end
2. **Brewed into the sputtering phase** — uncondensed steam pushed through the coffee
3. **Grind too fine** — building excessive pressure and temperature
4. **Left coffee in hot pot** — didn't pour immediately after brewing

Fix sequence: lower the flame → stop earlier (or cold-tap sooner) → grind slightly coarser → pour immediately

---

### "Coffee tastes sour, thin, or weak"

Most likely causes:
1. **Low yield** — not enough water pushed through the coffee before stopping
2. **Grind too coarse** — insufficient extraction
3. **Underfilled basket** — not enough coffee
4. **Starting with cold water** — early brew temperature too low

Fix sequence: check yield (aim for 130–150ml) → grind finer → fill basket fully → use freshly boiled water

---

### "Brew stopped early / stalled"

Most likely causes:
1. **Heat cut too soon** — not enough residual energy to finish the brew
2. **Grind too coarse** — very low resistance, pressure discharged quickly
3. **No diffusion plate** — heat drops sharply when gas is cut

Fix: briefly reapply low heat for 10–15 seconds → consider a diffusion plate

---

### "Pot sputtering very early (low yield)"

Most likely causes:
1. **Heat too high** — boiler overheated quickly
2. **Grind too fine** — excessive pressure build-up
3. **Overfilled boiler** — too much water heated aggressively

Fix: use lowest flame → grind slightly coarser → for dark roasts, fill boiler to 2/3 only

---

### "Leaking around the join"

Most likely cause:
- Gasket degraded, cracked, or hardened
- Pot not screwed together tightly enough

Fix: inspect gasket — if it's hard, cracked, or visibly deteriorated, replace it. Bialetti 4-cup replacement gaskets are cheap and widely available.

---

### "Coffee tastes fine but just too strong"

This is normal for Moka coffee. Try adding 80–100ml of freshly boiled water to your cup (a "Moka Americano"). This typically:
- Reduces intensity to a pleasant level
- Reveals sweetness that was hidden under concentration
- Balances the acidity beautifully

Many people prefer Moka coffee this way, especially with lighter roasted coffees.

## Notes on Consistency

The Moka pot is surprisingly sensitive. Small changes produce noticeable results:
- A different coffee bag from the same roaster can need a grind adjustment
- Back-to-back brews on the same day may differ because the pot is already warm
- Seasonal temperature changes can affect timing

This is not a failure — it's the nature of the brewer. The more brews you log, the faster you'll adapt to these variations.
